AsterNav

Autonomous Aerial Robot Navigation in Darkness Using Passive Computation. A monocular, structured-light-based depth estimation and navigation pipeline for tiny quadrotors operating in absolute darkness.

Overview

Search and rescue operations in disaster zones often take place in absolute darkness due to power outages or underground structural collapse. While tiny quadrotors are ideally suited to traverse these cramped and dangerous environments to locate survivors, they are severely resource-constrained and cannot carry heavy sensors or compute modules.

AsterNav solves this problem by combining an active Infrared (IR) structured light source with a monocular IR camera fitted with a large-aperture coded lens. This creates a passive computational depth cue, which enables real-time navigation around unknown obstacles without external positioning infrastructure like GPS or motion capture.

Core Research Question Investigated:
"How can tiny, resource-constrained aerial robots estimate depth and navigate obstacles safely in absolute darkness using only monocular sensing and onboard computation?"
Conceptual Diagram: Monocular IR coded aperture depth estimation and navigation setup in absolute darkness.

Coded Aperture & PSF

Traditional lenses discard depth information by projecting 3D points onto a single 2D sensor plane. AsterNav introduces a coded aperture lens that deliberately shapes the incoming light rays.

By blocking specific ray directions, the coded lens yields a depth-dependent blur pattern, also known as the Point Spread Function (PSF). As an obstacle moves closer or further from the camera, the structured light points projected on it deform and scale into specific coded patterns. This acts as a passive optical signature for depth.

  • The coded aperture preserves high-frequency details that traditional defocus lenses lose.
  • The shape, orientation, and scale of the PSF directly correspond to physical depth.
  • A deep neural network (AsterNet) can easily learn these optical features to predict accurate distance values.

Optics Calibration Bank

To bridge the gap between simulation and real-world deployment, we capture a physical Calibration Bank of Point Spread Functions.

We place calibration targets at precise sub-millimeter intervals along the optical axis, capturing the structured light reflection at every depth step. This empirical bank models exact lens distortions, sensor noise, and aberration effects that are otherwise mathematically intractable to model.

  • Compiles a comprehensive set of real-world optical responses across the system's operating range.
  • Ensures the deep model is calibrated for minor lens alignment imperfections.
  • Forms the foundational transfer function used to render high-fidelity synthetic datasets.

Synthetic Dataset Generation

Training deep networks for depth estimation usually requires thousands of manually labeled real-world images, which is near-impossible to gather in absolute darkness.

We developed an automated simulation pipeline (datagen.py) that utilizes our physical PSF calibration bank to synthesize infinite labeled training images. The pipeline projects structured light patterns onto arbitrary shapes from base datasets, applies the depth-dependent PSF distortions, and simulates sensor noise.

  • Randomized polygonal obstacles are placed at arbitrary distances to simulate cluttered rescue zones.
  • Enables zero-shot sim-to-real transfer: models trained in simulation deploy directly on real hardware.
  • Zero human annotations required for training.

Deployment & Performance

The entire depth estimation and planning pipeline was integrated onto a custom quadrotor platform running on an NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano. AsterNet successfully performs monocular structured-light depth estimation onboard in real-time at 20 Hz.

In real-world testing, the quadrotor successfully navigated around unknown obstacles of various geometries, materials, and colors in absolute darkness. It achieved an overall success rate of 95.5%, successfully avoiding challenging targets including dark matte surfaces (which absorb IR light) and extremely thin ropes of only 6.25mm diameter.

Limitations & Future Work

  • Range Limitations: Active structured light projector power limits the maximum depth range to near-to-mid distances.
  • Structured Light Overlap: In multi-robot teams, overlapping structured light patterns from other agents can cause depth estimation interference.
  • Dynamic Obstacles: The planner assumes obstacles are static, requiring further integration of temporary occupancy grids for dynamic avoidance.
